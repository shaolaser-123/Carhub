Engines

Combustion Engines

Combustion engines are the heart of traditional performance cars and still dominate gearhead culture. They work by burning fuel and air inside cylinders to drive pistons, which turn a crankshaft and send power to the wheels. The magic is in the sound and response: a V8 rumble, a turbo whistle, or a high revving VTEC kick at 8000 rpm. Whether it’s a simple inline-4 or a flat-plane V8, combustion gives you mechanical feedback, heat, vibration, and character that EVs can’t fake. Maintenance means oil changes, spark plugs, and timing belts, but the payoff is analog driving feel and decades of tuning potential.

Horsepower

Horsepower is how fast an engine can do work, and it’s what wins drag races and bragging rights. Think of it as top-end speed: it’s calculated from torque and rpm, so high-revving engines like the S2000’s F20C make big hp numbers even with modest torque. 400 hp in a 3000 lb car feels violent, while 400 hp in a 5000 lb SUV feels fine. More hp means faster quarter mile times and higher top speed, but without traction or gearing it’s just noise. Gearheads care because hp decides if you’re pulling on the highway or getting gapped by a tuned Civic.

Torque

Torque is the grunt that shoves you back in your seat. It’s rotational force, what actually gets a car moving from a stop and makes passing effortless without downshifting. Diesels and big V8s make torque down low, which is why a Mustang GT feels punchy at 2000 rpm while a Honda needs 6000 rpm to wake up. EVs cheat with instant torque from 0 rpm, but in gas cars, torque curve matters: a flat curve means usable power everywhere, a peaky curve means you live in high rpm. More torque means better towing, faster launches, and that addictive push gearheads live for.

EV Batteries

EV batteries are the fuel tank of electric cars, usually big lithium-ion packs under the floor. They store energy measured in kWh, which decides range: 60 kWh gets you ∼200 miles, 100 kWh pushes 300+ miles. The trade-off is weight. Battery packs are heavy, so EVs handle flat and planted but feel numb compared to light gas cars. Charging speed depends on voltage architecture, with 800V systems like Hyundai’s adding 200 miles in 18 minutes on DC fast chargers. Degradation, cold weather range loss, and fire risk are real quirks, but instant torque and zero maintenance balance it for many drivers.
